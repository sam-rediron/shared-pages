Operating Rhythm:
How the PRDs Deliver Jason’s Vision

Jason Boldt authored the Moments PRD v0.1 (May 2026), making the case that ImpactOS needs connective tissue between metrics, tasks, documents, and meetings — organized around the school operating rhythm rather than Charter Impact’s service categories. This document explains how the current PRD architecture delivers every goal from that document, and why the implementation approach differs from the original proposal.

Jason’s diagnosis is correct. His goals are all met. The implementation architecture is different.

Jason proposed “Moments” as a first-class product object — a navigable entity with its own routes, database tables, and UI section that orchestrates over the existing calendar, dashboard, and document features.

The PRDs instead implement the same functionality as enhancements to the existing calendar, dashboard, and document surfaces. The operating rhythm is a design philosophy that makes every feature smarter — not a separate feature that sits alongside them.

The end result for users is identical. EDs never navigate to “My Moments” — they just find the right information at the right time because every surface was designed with the operating rhythm in mind.

Goal Mapping

Jason’s 5 Goals — Where Each One Lives

1

“Make ImpactOS the system of record for the CI ↔ school operating rhythm”

  • Calendar events gain meeting-type metadata, linked metrics, linked documents, and participant tracking
  • Every recurring touchpoint (weekly CI meeting, board meeting) is configured as a recurring meeting series by the DOCF
  • Meeting records capture an immutable snapshot of what was discussed, decided, and assigned — the institutional record
  • The DOCF configures the operating rhythm once per school; it runs itself on cadence
Delivered by PRD-18 PRD-4
2

“Deliver the right data, tasks, and docs to the right people at the right time”

  • Setback rules fire automatically before each meeting: digest emails go out 24 hours before, prep tasks are created 5 days before, in-app notifications fire at configurable offsets
  • The dashboard shows an “Upcoming Meeting” widget above priority KPIs — meeting name, type, time, host, and the top 3 configured metrics with current values
  • Documents are organized by meeting context and surface in the live meeting right pane
  • Calendar shows meeting events alongside compliance tasks with prep status indicators
Delivered by PRD-3 PRD-4 PRD-5 PRD-18
3

“Preserve institutional memory”

  • Every meeting produces an immutable MeetingRecord on close: full agenda, all decisions, all action items, metric values as-of close time, and document references
  • Meeting records are searchable and filterable by type, date range, participants, school, and decision tags
  • Records survive DOCF turnover and ED transitions. A new DOCF can review the last 6 months of meeting records to ramp on each school
  • Metric snapshots store values as-of close time, not current values — critical for charter renewal and authorizer engagements
Delivered by PRD-18
4

“Enable DOCFs to operate proactively, not reactively”

  • Meeting templates provide consistent starting configurations: default cadence, attendees, metrics, agenda, and setback rules
  • DOCFs apply a template to a school in under 3 minutes — set cadence, attendees, metrics, and the series runs itself
  • The DOCF console shows a “Today / This Week” meeting triage view across their entire portfolio with prep status indicators
  • Setback rules generate prep tasks and digests automatically — no manual email coordination
Delivered by PRD-8 PRD-18
5

“Support multi-site CMO operators natively”

  • A single meeting can span all schools in an organization with per-school metric columns
  • Organizations are a first-class entity in the data model — every school belongs to one, and CMOs group schools naturally
  • The school selector groups schools by parent organization throughout the product
  • Meeting records store per-school metric snapshots for cross-org meetings
Delivered by PRD-2 PRD-18

Architecture Decisions

What Changed From Jason’s Proposal (and Why)

Jason’s Proposal Current PRD Approach Why
“Moments” as a first-class product object with its own routes (/moments/*) Meeting lifecycle features enhance the calendar, dashboard, and documents — no separate section EDs think in terms of “my board meeting” and “my weekly check-in,” not “my Moments.” The product should speak their language.
“My Moments” as an ED navigation item Upcoming Meeting widget on the dashboard + meeting events on the calendar EDs shouldn’t have to learn a new product concept to find their next meeting. It’s already where they look.
MomentTemplate / MomentSeries / MomentInstance entity hierarchy MeetingTemplate / RecurringSeries / enhanced CalendarEvent Same data model, different naming. Calendar events are the anchor entity — the concept EDs already understand.
/moments/{id}/live as a dedicated surface Live meeting surface accessible from calendar event detail Same UI surface, accessed from the calendar rather than a separate route hierarchy.
MomentRecord as a separate entity MeetingRecord — immutable artifact generated on meeting close Same functionality, same immutability. Just named plainly.
“Moments” as the GTM narrative “Charter Impact embedded in your operating rhythm” The GTM story is the same — the product noun is just plainer. No asking EDs to learn what a “Moment” is.

The Net

Everything Jason wants is built. The connective tissue between metrics, tasks, documents, and meetings exists. The operating rhythm drives the product. The institutional record survives turnover. DOCFs configure once and the system runs itself. Pre-meeting digests replace email chaos.

The only thing that changed is the packaging: instead of a standalone “Moments” feature that orchestrates over the existing surfaces, the existing surfaces are themselves rhythm-aware. The calendar knows about meeting types and setback automation. The dashboard knows what meeting is next. Documents organize around meetings. The connective tissue is invisible — which is what makes it feel native rather than bolted on.
